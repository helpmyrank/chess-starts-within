How a chapter actually reads

From the chapter "Remain Confident" — on the games that look hopeless and aren't.

Chapter 3 — Remain Confident
Chess games are won and lost right up until the very last move.

The internal voice that says "this is over" is often wrong — sometimes embarrassingly so, when the game is nowhere near decided. The problem is that voice can sound very certain, and certainty is easy to mistake for accuracy. It's a feeling about the position, not an objective assessment of it.

Most players don't explicitly decide to give up mid-game. What happens instead is subtler: they stop trying as hard. The moves get quicker and less considered. The question shifts from "is there a way out of this?" to "how do I make this loss as painless as possible?" That shift is often what actually decides the game.

— Chess Starts Within, p. 16
The approach

Three things this book keeps coming back to

Emotional control

Notice it, don't suppress it

Frustration, anxiety, overconfidence — the goal isn't feeling nothing. It's recognizing what you feel at the board without letting it choose your next move.

Patience

Active, not passive

Patience here isn't waiting for something to happen. It's improving your position while resisting the urge to force a result the board isn't ready for.

Self-awareness

The board as a mirror

How you handle a blunder, a winning position, a long quiet middlegame — tends to look a lot like how you handle the rest of your life under pressure.
